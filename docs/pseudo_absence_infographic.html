🔥 Wildfire ML · Training Data

Why Pseudo-Absence Generation
is Needed

Wildfire prediction models are binary classifiers. They need examples of both fire and non-fire conditions. But real-world fire databases only record where fires happened — not where they didn't.

The Fundamental Data Problem
🗺️

What We Have

GPS-recorded fire ignition points only. No "non-fire" records exist in any database.

→
🤖

What the Model Needs

Binary classifiers require both classes: fire (1) and confirmed no-fire (0) to learn a decision boundary.

→
❓

The Gap

A location with no fire record might simply be unobserved — not truly fire-free. True absences are unknowable.

⚠️
Core Problem

True absences don't exist — so we must generate
pseudo-absences as training controls

Pseudo-absences are synthetic "no-fire" points inserted as the negative class. They make training possible — but their quality determines whether the final model is valid or deeply biased. Poor placement doesn't just reduce accuracy; it produces a model that appears to work but fails silently on real predictions.

How to read the AUC quality test

A second classifier is trained specifically to distinguish fire locations from pseudo-absence controls. If it can easily separate them (AUC ≈ 0.90), the controls are unrealistic — they don't resemble fire-prone environments. If it can't (AUC ≈ 0.50, near random), the controls are ecologically valid and the training set is unbiased.

AUC Quality Scale
0.50 ✓ Ideal 0.90 ✗ Biased
🔑

The Bottom Line

Pseudo-absence placement is the single most consequential decision in wildfire model training. Random placement creates a biased training problem that is too easy — inflating performance metrics and producing a model that fails in deployment. Generating absences that match the environmental profile of fire-prone areas (via heuristics or GANs) forces the model to learn genuinely discriminative fire risk signals, yielding predictions that are ecologically valid and operationally reliable.
